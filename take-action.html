STEP 01 · MAP IT

STEP 02 · CONTACT
Message the decision-makers

Local officials control the contract; state legislators control the law. A short, polite, specific message from a constituent is remarkably effective. Start with the people who represent you.

STEP 03 · REQUEST RECORDS
File a public-records request

NC's public-records law lets you ask any agency for its Flock contract, data-sharing agreements, retention policy, and audit/search logs. What you get back is often the strongest evidence at a public meeting.

STEP 04 · SHOW UP
Speak at a public meeting

City council and county commission meetings have a public-comment period. Two minutes, a clear ask ("don't renew this contract"), and a couple of neighbors beside you changes votes. Check your locality's calendar for the next one.

Talking points you can borrow

Keep it short, factual and local. A few that land well in NC:

  • "This system tracks everyone by default — not suspects. A warrant is needed to search my house, but none is needed to log everywhere my car goes."
  • "Our data can be shared with agencies we've never heard of, including out-of-state and federal. Who audits that?"
  • "Neighboring NC communities — Hillsborough, Pittsboro, Chatham, Macon — reviewed the same product and walked away."
  • "What is the annual cost, and what independent evidence shows it reduces crime enough to justify blanket surveillance?"
  • "Who can search this data, how long is it kept, and how do residents verify the 90-day limit is being honored?"
  • "I'm asking the council to not renew this contract and to hold a public hearing before any expansion."
  • "Flock isn't just plate readers anymore — it's adding always-on microphones, cameras that follow people, and drones. Where does this expansion stop, and who signs off on it?"
  • "When New Hanover County released its audit logs, the names of every searching agency were redacted. If we can't see who searches our data, the 'safeguards' are unenforceable."
  • "These cameras get plates wrong — a misread put an innocent Fayetteville woman in handcuffs and cost the city $60,000. One wrong letter is all it takes."
  • "Where cities have actually audited these systems, it hasn't gone well: Austin found over 20% of searches lacked documentation or justification, and Santa Cruz cancelled 6–1 after discovering 4,000 federal searches of its local data. What would an audit here find?"

STEP 03, IN DETAIL

What to ask for in a records request

NC's Public Records Act (N.C.G.S. Chapter 132) entitles you to these documents. Four requests cover everything a council needs to see — the answers (and the refusals) both make powerful public comment.

01 · THE MONEY

The full Flock contract, all invoices and payments, and — crucially — the funding source. Seizure funds and federal grants often hide the true cost from the budget.

02 · THE RULES

Retention, access and sharing policies, plus audit/search logs for the past year. NC law requires a written policy before deployment — ask for it.

03 · THE FOOTPRINT

Camera counts and locations, any NCDOT encroachment permits, and expansion plans. Agencies sometimes run more cameras than they've disclosed.

04 · THE SHARING

Any agreements or correspondence about sharing with federal, out-of-state, or network-wide partners. This is where the "local tool" story usually falls apart.

COPY-AND-SEND STARTER LETTER
To the Public Records Custodian, [agency name]:

Under the North Carolina Public Records Act (N.C.G.S. Chapter 132), I request electronic copies of the following records concerning your agency's use of automated license plate readers (Flock Safety or any other vendor):

1. All current contracts, invoices, and payment records for ALPR products or services, including the funding source for each payment;
2. All policies governing ALPR data retention, access, and sharing, and any audit or search logs from the past 12 months;
3. The number and locations of ALPR cameras your agency operates or accesses, including any NCDOT encroachment permits; and
4. Any agreements, memoranda of understanding, or correspondence concerning ALPR data sharing with federal agencies, out-of-state agencies, or any multi-agency network.

If any portion of this request is denied, please cite the statutory basis for each withholding and release the remainder. Please confirm receipt of this request.

Thank you,
[your name, address, and email]

NC law requires agencies to respond "as promptly as possible" — there's no fixed deadline, so follow up politely after two weeks. Send by email to the agency's records custodian (call the front desk to get the right address).

TALK TO YOUR NEIGHBORS

Meet people where they are

Surveillance concerns cross party lines, but different openers land with different people. Lead with what your listener already cares about:

NEIGHBORS

"Did you know there's a camera on [street] logging every car that passes — including yours, every day? Want to see the map?"

PARENTS

"Flock employees have been caught watching live feeds of kids at a community center. These cameras record people, not just plates."

SMALL-GOVERNMENT CONSERVATIVES

"A private company tracks every driver for the government, paid with seizure funds that never faced a budget vote. Our constitution calls that a general warrant."

PROGRESSIVES

"This data flows to thousands of outside agencies — including federal ones — no matter what our town's policy says. Local control is an illusion here."

READY-TO-USE STRUCTURE

Your three-minute public comment

Most NC councils and commissions give each speaker about three minutes. Here's a structure that fits — fill in your own details and read it almost as-is.

0:00–0:30 · OPEN
Who you are

"Good evening. My name is [name], I live at [address], and I'm here about the Flock camera contract." Concern about these cameras crosses party lines — say so.

0:30–1:30 · THE PROBLEM
What the system does

Every plate scanned, almost none tied to any crime. Data searchable by thousands of outside agencies — audits released in NC had every searching agency's name redacted.

1:30–2:30 · MAKE IT LOCAL
Why it matters here

Name the local contract cost and camera count (from the agenda or a records request). Cite the NC misread cases — a $60,000 settlement in Fayetteville — and the towns that walked away.

2:30–3:00 · THE ASK
One specific request

"I'm asking you to pause any expansion and not renew this contract until there's a written oversight ordinance — retention limits, council approval for data-sharing, and public audits."

Meeting-night tips: arrive 15–30 minutes early to sign up (some boards cap speakers) · bring printed copies of your comment for the clerk to distribute · state your name and address for the record · practice once with a timer · if a member challenges you, stay calm and offer to follow up in writing · end on your specific ask.
WHEN THEY PUSH BACK

Answering the five most common defenses

You'll hear the same arguments at every meeting. Here's what they miss.

"IT ONLY PHOTOGRAPHS PLATES, NOT PEOPLE."

Flock's own patent filings describe classifying people — by clothing, height, gender, even facial features — and its Condor cameras already track people on camera. "Plates only" is where it started, not where it is.

"DATA IS ONLY KEPT 90 DAYS."

Deletion at 90 days doesn't limit who searches it first: a single query can sweep thousands of networks, and Flock has given federal agencies access to local data in other states without the local agency's knowledge. Retention limits aren't access limits.

"IT REDUCES CRIME."

Crime fell nationwide in 2025 — in cities with and without Flock. Austin's independent audit found no demonstrated causal link, and over 20% of its ALPR searches lacked documentation. Ask for independent local evidence, not vendor marketing.

"IT'S JUST A SECURITY CAMERA."

A security camera watches one property. A Flock network scans every driver on public roads, fingerprints their vehicles, and pools it into a nationally searchable database — no warrant, no notice, no opt-out. That's a dragnet, not a doorbell cam.

"IF YOU HAVE NOTHING TO HIDE, YOU HAVE NOTHING TO FEAR."

Jacqueline McNeill and Jasmine Horne had nothing to hide — NC drivers handcuffed and arrested over misreads. The Fourth Amendment protects everyone because innocence isn't a shield once a database says otherwise. And your pattern of life — church, clinic, protest, gun shop — is nobody's business to log.
