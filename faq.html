FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Questions we hear at every meeting

Straight answers about what these cameras do, what NC law allows, and what you can actually do about it. Something missing? Ask us.

What is a Flock camera, and what does it actually record?

Flock Safety sells pole-mounted, solar-powered cameras that photograph every vehicle that passes — no suspicion required. AI extracts the plate plus a "vehicle fingerprint": make, model, color, dents, bumper stickers, roof racks. The record goes into Flock's searchable cloud.

It's not just plates. The cameras trigger on any movement and can record people, and Flock's own patent filings describe classifying people by characteristics. Flock also sells always-on microphones (Raven), AI cameras that track people (Condor), and plate-reading drones (Aerodome). See The Cameras for the full stack.

Who can see the data from cameras in my town?

Far more people than your local police department. Agencies can search each other's Flock data across city, county, state and — in practice — federal lines. Audits obtained in NC show a single search can sweep thousands of camera networks at once, and NC A&T's 16 Greensboro cameras alone were searched 1.39 million times in three months by 3,487 agencies, mostly from out of state.

When New Hanover County released its audit logs, the name of every searching agency was redacted — so residents can't even see who is looking.

Is this legal? What does North Carolina law say?

NC has some guardrails: plate data generally can't be kept beyond 90 days, can't be used for routine traffic enforcement, is confidential and can't be sold (§ 20-183.32), and misuse is a Class 1 misdemeanor. NC is also the only state that has required Flock to hold a security-systems license.

But the gaps are big: no cap on how many cameras an agency can run, no limit on HOA or private deployments, and the 2026 state budget authorized plate readers on state-owned land statewide. Meanwhile courts are moving the other way — the Supreme Court's Carpenter and Chatrie decisions treat warrantless location tracking as a Fourth Amendment problem, and a direct challenge to a city Flock network (Schmidt v. Norfolk) is before the federal circuit that covers NC.

I have nothing to hide. Why should I care?

Two reasons. First, innocence hasn't protected people: Jacqueline McNeill was handcuffed in Fayetteville over a plate misread (the city paid a $60,000 settlement), Jasmine Horne was wrongfully arrested the same way, and a driver just over the SC line was held at gunpoint over a one-letter misread. One wrong character is all it takes.

Second, your pattern of life — home, work, church, clinic, protest, gun shop — is revealing even when every stop is lawful. NC's constitution bans "general warrants" precisely because search-everyone-first policing is dangerous to liberty, whoever you are.

Don't the cameras reduce crime?

The evidence is much weaker than the sales pitch. Fewer than 1% of scanned vehicles are ever tied to a crime. Austin's independent audit found no demonstrated causal link between its Flock network and crime reduction — and found over 20% of searches lacked documentation. Crime fell nationally in 2025 in cities with and without Flock.

Communities that reviewed the trade-off — Hillsborough, Pittsboro, Chatham, Macon here in NC, and dozens more nationwide — decided it wasn't worth it. Ask for independent local evidence, not vendor marketing.

Can I opt out of being scanned?

No. There is no opt-out — if you drive past a camera on a public road, you're logged. That's a core part of the problem: the only real "opt-out" is a community deciding not to deploy the system, or removing it. That decision belongs to your local elected officials — and they answer to you.

How do I find out if there are cameras near me?

Check the crowdsourced DeFlock camera map — and add any you spot that aren't on it. You can also search published audit logs at Have I Been Flocked?, check Flock's own transparency portals, or file a public-records request with your local agency (templates in the toolbox).

Is DeFlock NC anti-police?

No. Wanting oversight of a mass-surveillance network is not anti-police — it's basic accountability, and it crosses party lines. Some of the most notable caution has come from law enforcement itself: Granville County Sheriff Robert Fountain pulled a Flock proposal from his own county's agenda over Fourth Amendment concerns. We advocate only through lawful means: public meetings, records requests, petitions, elections and the press — never tampering with equipment.

What's the single most useful thing I can do right now?

Three things, in about ten minutes: sign the petition, email your legislators, and check when your town council or county commission next meets. A room full of neighbors asking hard questions is how every NC cancellation so far has happened. The full playbook is in Take Action.
