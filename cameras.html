THE TECHNOLOGY

How Flock cameras work — and where they are in NC

Automated license plate readers (ALPRs) are not "just traffic cameras." They are a networked, searchable, always-on record of who drives where — and plate readers are only the start of Flock's fast-growing surveillance suite. Here's what it all captures, what NC law allows, and where it's already been installed.

What a single camera captures

01 · CAPTURE

A photo of every passing vehicle — day or night — regardless of whether any crime is suspected.

02 · READ

AI extracts the plate plus a "vehicle fingerprint": make, model, color, dents, bumper stickers, roof racks.

03 · STORE

Records are uploaded to Flock's cloud. NC law caps retention at 90 days — longer with a preservation request or warrant.

04 · SHARE

Agencies can search each other's data across city, county, state — and, in some cases, federal — lines.

The core problem: a warrant is required to search your home, but no warrant is required to build a minute-by-minute map of everywhere your car has been. Multiply one camera by a statewide network and you have a mass-surveillance system that tracks everyone by default — the innocent included.

How far your data travels

"Local" cameras don't stay local. Flock sells agencies access to its national lookup — marketed as reaching billions of additional plate reads per month — so a single search can sweep thousands of camera networks at once. Audit logs released under NC's public-records law show how far NC data actually travels:

IT'S NOT JUST PLATE READERS

Flock is building a full surveillance stack

License plate readers were just the beginning. Flock Safety now sells a connected suite that can see, hear, and follow — much of it solar-powered and installable without wiring or permits, and all of it feeding the same searchable cloud.

RAVEN · AUDIO
Microphones that listen

Solar-powered audio sensors sold for "gunshot detection" — they analyze sound in 5-second clips and triangulate a location to within ~90 feet. In late 2025 Flock began expanding them to listen for human voices and "sounds of distress."

CONDOR · PTZ
Cameras that follow you

AI pan-tilt-zoom cameras that detect and track people. In a June 2026 demo, a Condor pivoted and zoomed to follow a passing reporter — despite Flock's claim that its cameras "don't track people." Dozens were also left exposed on the open internet.

AERODOME · DRONES
Drones overhead

After buying drone-maker Aerodome, Flock now offers "Drone as First Responder" fleets with self-charging docks — plus in-flight license plate reading, vehicle-follow, and beyond-line-of-sight flight, targeting ~100 cities.

And the fine print keeps moving: Flock's patent filings describe classifying people by clothing, height, gender and facial features — not just plates. Security researchers have documented 51 flaws in its devices, and the company rewrote its terms of service twice in three months (147 changes). What a town approves today is not what it will be running next year.

Sources: EFF (Raven audio) · InvestigateTV (Condor) · Independent Institute (exposed cameras) · Flock (Aerodome) · 48 Hills

What North Carolina law actually says

NC has some guardrails — and some serious gaps. Key provisions from Article 3D of Chapter 20 and Session Law 2023-151:

GUARDRAILS ON THE BOOKS
  • Captured plate data generally can't be retained beyond 90 days without a preservation request or court order.
  • ALPRs may not be used to enforce routine traffic violations.
  • Agencies must adopt written policies on retention, sharing, training, auditing, security and oversight before deploying.
  • Misusing ALPR data is a Class 1 misdemeanor.
  • Captured plate data is confidential and may not be sold (§ 20-183.32).
  • NC is the only state that has required Flock to hold a security-systems license — a Wake County judge halted its installs in 2023 until it got one.
THE GAPS
  • No statewide cap on how many cameras an agency can run or where private HOAs/businesses may point them.
  • The 2026 budget authorizes NCDOT to place readers on state-owned land statewide, in coordination with the SBI.
  • The SBI highway pilot grew to ~140 cameras across 32 agencies, logging 150M+ scans — with a report to the General Assembly due Oct 1, 2026.
  • Enforcement of the rules is largely self-policed by the agencies that benefit from the data.

Sources: NC Gen. Stat. Ch. 20, Art. 3D · WRAL · ACLU of NC

THIS ISN'T HYPOTHETICAL

Misreads have already hurt North Carolinians

A plate camera only has to be wrong once. When a "hit" is wrong, officers act on it — and innocent drivers end up in handcuffs.

FAYETTEVILLE, NC
Jacqueline McNeill

Arrested after camera images wrongly tied her white sedan to a drive-by shooting — police had captured two different cars. Fayetteville paid a $60,000 settlement and a written apology from the chief.

CHARLOTTE, NC
Jasmine Horne

A second-grade teacher surrounded by 10 CMPD cars, gun drawn, outside her grandmother's home — after officers put the wrong person's name into a plate-reader alert. CMPD later changed its policies.

YORK COUNTY, SC
Held at gunpoint over one letter

Just across the NC line, a camera misread a single character and grandparents were held at gunpoint in front of their three-year-old granddaughter; a second driver, Steven Melvin, is suing over a similar one-letter misread stop.

GREENVILLE, SC
Two sisters wrongly arrested

Two women were arrested after a Flock hit wrongly flagged their rental car as stolen. They're suing — one of a growing number of cases just over the NC line.

And the courts are catching up

The legal ground under warrantless location tracking is shifting — including in the federal circuit that covers North Carolina. And NC's own constitution declares that "general warrants… are dangerous to liberty and shall not be granted" (Art. I, § 20) — the founding-era name for blanket authority to search everyone in hopes of finding something.

U.S. SUPREME COURT · 2018
Carpenter v. United States

Accessing historical location data is a Fourth Amendment search, because it reveals "the whole of [a person's] physical movements."

U.S. SUPREME COURT · JUNE 29, 2026
Chatrie v. United States

Held 6–3: pulling a person's location-history data from Google is a Fourth Amendment search — you keep a reasonable expectation of privacy in your movements even when a private company holds the data. The same logic ALPR networks depend on.

4TH CIRCUIT · PENDING — COVERS NC
Schmidt v. City of Norfolk

A direct Fourth Amendment challenge to a city-wide Flock ALPR network, now before the federal appeals court whose rulings bind North Carolina.

Sources: CBS17 (McNeill settlement) · WCNC (Horne detention & CMPD policy changes) · Institute for Justice (nationwide misread cases, incl. York County; IJ also litigates Schmidt v. City of Norfolk) · Supreme Court opinion, Chatrie v. United States · SCOTUSblog.

HAPPENING NOW · STATEWIDE

How NC locked ALPRs onto its highways

The most consequential surveillance decision in North Carolina didn't happen at a town council — it happened in Raleigh, and it nearly slipped by unnoticed. Here's the timeline.

2023
The pilot begins

Session Law 2023-151 lets the SBI run a pilot placing plate readers on state highways and roads — with a built-in expiration of July 1, 2026.

Spring 2026
Push to make it permanent

With the clock running out, House Bill 206 is advanced to make the SBI highway program permanent. By then the pilot had ~140 cameras, 32 agencies, 150M+ scans.

June 17, 2026
Stripped out — then slipped back

The plate-reader language is removed from HB 206. But the same authority is quietly folded into the state budget instead.

July 2026 · NOW LAW
Authorized statewide

The budget passes, letting NCDOT place ALPRs on state-owned land with the SBI — across highways and "all other state roads." No standalone vote, no sunset.

Two 2026 deadlines worth watching: Flock's NC security-systems license runs through August 31, 2026 and must be renewed — NC is the only state to require one, after a Wake County judge halted Flock's unlicensed installs in 2023. And a report on the SBI pilot is due to the General Assembly by Oct 1, 2026 — a natural pressure point to demand limits.

Sources: WRAL · WECT · Axios Raleigh · ACLU of NC

FOLLOW THE MONEY

What all of this costs you

Flock leases its cameras at roughly $3,000 per camera per year, plus installation fees. Multiply that across the 700+ verified Flock cameras in North Carolina and taxpayers are sending millions of public dollars a year to a private surveillance vendor — often without a single line-item budget vote.
